Abstract: The majority of algorithms now in use for automatic 3D morphometry of human brain MRI scans are made for data with near-isotropic voxels at a resolution of about 1 mm. They also often have contrast constraints, which usually necessitate T1 scans (e.g., MP-RAGE). Due to this restriction, millions of MRI images obtained in clinical settings each year with substantial inter-slice spacing ("thick slice") cannot be analyzed. The use of quantitative neuroimaging in healthcare is hampered by the inability to quantitatively interpret these images, which also prevents research projects that may achieve large sample sizes and significantly advance our knowledge of the human brain. Recent developments in CNNs are yielding exceptional outcomes in MRI contrast synthesis and super-resolution.
Nevertheless, these methods do not generalize to different clinical acquisition protocols—even within sites—because they are highly sensitive to the contrast, resolution, and orientation of the input pictures. Here, we introduce SynthSR, a CNN training technique that generates an isotropic scan of canonical contrast (usually a 1 mm MP-RAGE) from one or more thick-slice scans with varying contrast, resolution, and orientation. Preprocessing, such as bias field correction or skull stripping, is not necessary for the method that is being discussed.
Importantly, SynthSR may be used to train CNNs for any combination of contrasts, resolutions, and orientations without high-resolution training data because it trains on synthetic input images created from 3D segmentations. We test the SynthSR-generated images in a variety of common downstream analyses and demonstrate their reliability for subcortical segmentation and volumetry, image registration (e.g., for tensor-based morphometry), and even cortical thickness morphometry, provided certain image quality requirements are satisfied. The source code can be found at github.com/BBillot/SynthSR.
